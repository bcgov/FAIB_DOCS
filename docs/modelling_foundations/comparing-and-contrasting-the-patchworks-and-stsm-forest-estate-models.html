12 Comparing and Contrasting the Patchworks and STSM Forest Estate Models

12.1 Introduction

Forest estate modelling is the process of projecting how a forest resource will develop over time under alternative management strategies, natural disturbances, and policy constraints. It provides a quantitative framework for evaluating the long-term consequences of management decisions on timber supply, forest condition, ecological values, and economic outcomes. A forest estate model typically combines information on current forest inventory, growth and yield, harvesting practices, land-base assumptions, and management objectives to simulate future forest conditions across decades or even centuries. The primary goal is to determine whether desired harvest levels and management objectives can be sustained while maintaining the productive capacity of the forest.

The two prominent systems used in BC forestry are Spatial Planning System’s Patchworks and the Golland’s SELES based Spatial Timber Supply Model (STSM). While both are designed and have been used to support forest management decision-making (STSM has been a co-development collaboration between it’s architect Dr.Andrew Fall and the BC the Ministry of Forests’ Forest Analysis and Inventory Branch), they differ substantially in architecture, analytical philosophy, modelling approach, and user interaction. Patchworks emphasizes interactive optimization and harvest scheduling, whereas STSM focuses on landscape dynamics simulation and cumulative effects assessment within the SELES modelling framework. Despite these differences, both platforms share a common objective: supporting sustainable forest management through spatially explicit analysis.

At a fundamental level, both systems are spatially explicit forest planning tools. Patchworks is described as a sustainable forest management optimization model that develops spatial harvest allocations while balancing multiple management objectives. It employs a goal-programming approach in which the system searches for harvest schedules that best satisfy user-defined objectives and constraints. STSM, in contrast, is a spatially explicit forest landscape and timber supply model implemented within the SELES (Spatially Explicit Landscape Event Simulator) framework. Rather than primarily seeking an optimal solution, STSM simulates landscape processes over time, including forest growth, harvesting, road development, succession, and natural disturbances.

12.1.1 Modelling Philosophy

One of the most significant differences between the two systems is their modelling philosophy. Patchworks is fundamentally an meta-heuristic (Meta-heuristics are high-level, problem-independent algorithmic frameworks designed to efficiently explore massive search spaces and find “good enough” solutions when exact methods take too long) goal programming model that attempts to optimize. Its scheduler continuously searches for improved harvest allocations and adjusts management activities to better satisfy a weighted objective function. Users can modify constraints and target weights while the model runs, and the optimization engine responds dynamically to these changes. This design allows planners to conduct trade-off analyses among timber supply, economics, visual quality, habitat, transportation, and other objectives.

STSM, by comparison, is fundamentally a simulation platform. It represents landscape dynamics through sequential landscape events that modify a shared spatio-temporal state space. Processes such as stand aging, timber growth, harvesting, road building, and wildfire occur according to specified rules and parameters. Instead of seeking a mathematical optimum, STSM evaluates the consequences of alternative management scenarios through repeated simulation runs. Timber supply estimates emerge from experimentation and iterative adjustment of harvest targets rather than from direct optimization.

Another important philosophical distinction between the two platforms concerns how timber supply is estimated and evaluated. In Patchworks, timber supply is typically treated as an optimization objective. The model actively searches for harvest schedules that achieve wood supply targets while simultaneously satisfying other constraints related to economics, transportation, habitat, visual quality, and forest structure. The optimization engine attempts to identify the harvest allocation that provides the best overall balance among competing objectives, meaning timber supply is determined directly through the optimization process. In this framework, the model seeks an efficient solution to a planning problem, with the resulting harvest flow representing the best schedule available under the specified assumptions and constraints.

STSM approaches the problem differently. Rather than directly solving for an optimal harvest level, sustainable timber supply emerges from the simulation of long-term landscape dynamics. Analysts begin by specifying a harvest target and then evaluate whether that target can be sustained over time. Key questions include whether the harvest level can be achieved in every period, whether growing stock remains stable or increases over the long term, and whether the landscape remains sustainable over centuries of simulation. If harvest targets cannot be met or growing stock declines, the harvest level is reduced; conversely, if growing stock continues to accumulate, higher harvest levels may be tested. Through repeated simulation runs and iterative binary-search procedures, STSM gradually converges on a sustainable harvest flow. This process resembles a scientific experiment in which a harvest hypothesis is proposed, tested through simulation, evaluated based on outcomes, adjusted, and retested until a sustainable long-term solution is identified. Timber supply is therefore not directly optimized but instead emerges from understanding how the forest responds to management actions and ecological processes through time.

12.1.2 Planning Horizon Perspective: Whole-Horizon Optimization versus Step-Wise Simulation

One of the most fundamental differences between Patchworks and STSM is how they view and evaluate time across the planning horizon. Patchworks is designed as an optimization model that evaluates the entire planning horizon simultaneously when searching for a solution. Rather than making decisions one period at a time, the scheduler considers conditions, objectives, constraints, and targets across all future periods while constructing a harvest schedule. This allows the model to identify harvest allocations that not only meet short-term objectives but also support long-term goals throughout the planning horizon. A harvesting decision made in Period 1 may be influenced by consequences that occur many decades later because the optimization process is attempting to find the best overall allocation across the entire planning horizon rather than maximizing performance in any single period.

This whole-horizon perspective allows Patchworks to effectively anticipate future constraints and opportunities. For example, the scheduler may choose to defer harvesting a highly productive stand in the near term because it recognizes that the stand will be needed to satisfy future harvest flow requirements, habitat targets, transportation objectives, or old-forest retention goals later in the planning horizon. Similarly, decisions regarding road construction, mill allocations, and visual quality objectives can be evaluated in terms of their impacts on all future periods simultaneously. As a result, the harvest schedule produced by Patchworks represents a globally optimized solution that attempts to balance competing objectives throughout the entire planning horizon.

STSM approaches the passage of time very differently. Rather than evaluating the entire planning horizon at once, STSM simulates forest development sequentially through a series of time steps. Each period begins with the current state of the landscape and then applies a sequence of landscape events such as stand aging, succession, natural disturbance, harvest planning, logging, road development, and reporting. The outputs from one time step become the starting conditions for the next. Consequently, STSM does not directly search for an optimal future harvest schedule across all periods simultaneously. Instead, future conditions emerge through the cumulative effects of management actions and ecological processes operating over time.

This step-wise simulation approach mirrors the way real forest ecosystems evolve. Forest conditions at Year 100 cannot be known directly at the beginning of a simulation; they emerge from what occurs during Years 1–99. Wildfire, insect outbreaks, road development, harvesting, regeneration, and succession all alter the landscape in ways that influence future opportunities and constraints. The model therefore evaluates how management strategies perform under dynamic landscape conditions rather than searching for a mathematically optimal allocation. Harvest sustainability is assessed by repeatedly simulating the future and observing outcomes such as harvest target achievement, growing-stock trends, age-class distributions, and landscape condition.

A useful analogy is that Patchworks resembles a chess player attempting to evaluate the entire game before making a move. The optimization engine examines future consequences throughout the planning horizon and selects actions that contribute to the best overall outcome. STSM, on the other hand, resembles actually playing the game move by move, with each move creating new conditions that influence subsequent decisions. The model does not know exactly what the future landscape will look like until it progresses through the sequence of ecological and management processes that produce it.

These differing views of time have important implications for forest management analysis. Patchworks excels at identifying efficient, balanced harvest schedules and evaluating trade-offs among competing objectives across an entire planning horizon. STSM excels at understanding how forests evolve through time, how disturbances and management actions interact, and how sustainability emerges from long-term ecological dynamics. In practice, Patchworks provides a powerful strategic optimization framework, whereas STSM provides a powerful landscape simulation framework for evaluating the long-term consequences of management decisions and cumulative effects under changing conditions.

12.1.3 Stochastic and Deterministic Modelling in Patchworks and STSM

An important consideration when comparing Patchworks and STSM is the role of deterministic and stochastic modelling in representing forest dynamics and estimating sustainable timber supply. Deterministic models produce the same result whenever the same inputs, assumptions, and parameters are used. In contrast, stochastic models incorporate elements of randomness, meaning that repeated simulations with identical starting conditions may produce different outcomes. Both Patchworks and STSM can be used within deterministic planning frameworks, but STSM was specifically designed to support varying levels of stochasticity and uncertainty in landscape processes.

Patchworks is largely deterministic in its harvest scheduling philosophy. Given a particular forest inventory, set of growth and yield assumptions, transportation network, management objectives, and constraint structure, the model seeks to identify an optimal or near-optimal harvest schedule. While multiple solutions may be explored by changing objectives, weights, or assumptions, the underlying objective is to determine a best allocation for the specified conditions. STSM incorporates both deterministic and stochastic capabilities, making it particularly useful for representing complex ecological systems where uncertainty is inherent. Many natural processes such as wildfire occurrence, insect outbreaks, disturbance spread, regeneration success, and harvest sequencing can be affected by stochastic processes. STSM allows the user to control the level of stochasticity through random number generator seeds and simulation settings, enabling either fully repeatable deterministic runs or multiple stochastic realizations of the same scenario. By running ensembles of simulations, analysts can evaluate not only the expected future condition of the landscape but also the range of possible outcomes.

While Patchworks is generally characterized as a deterministic optimization model, there is an important stochastic element embedded within its optimization approach through its use of heuristic search methods related to simulated annealing and stochastic improvement algorithms. Patchworks’ scheduler does not simply solve a linear programming problem and return a single exact mathematical optimum. Instead, it continuously searches a very large solution space of possible harvest schedules. To avoid becoming trapped in local optima, the scheduler introduces controlled randomness into the search process, allowing it to explore alternative allocations that may initially appear inferior but ultimately lead to better overall solutions. This is conceptually similar to simulated annealing, where occasional acceptance of less favorable moves helps the algorithm escape local peaks and valleys in the objective-function landscape.

The practical effect is that two runs of Patchworks using identical datasets, objectives, and constraints may not always converge on exactly the same harvest schedule. The differences are often small, particularly after the scheduler has been allowed sufficient time to stabilize, but the model may discover different spatial harvest patterns that achieve very similar objective-function values. This is especially common when many stands have similar eligibility, value, or priority characteristics and multiple feasible solutions exist. In such cases, the stochastic search process helps identify alternative schedules that may be nearly equivalent from an optimization perspective but differ spatially.

This stochastic search component has several benefits:

  • Avoiding local optima: The scheduler is less likely to become trapped in a harvest pattern that is only locally optimal.
  • Finding better global solutions: Broader exploration of the solution space can reveal harvest schedules that would not be found using a purely deterministic search.
  • Generating alternative solutions: Multiple model runs may produce alternative schedules with similar objective achievement, which can be useful for stakeholder discussions and operational planning.
  • Supporting sensitivity analysis: Small differences between runs can help identify portions of the landscape where management flexibility exists versus areas that are consistently selected by the optimizer.

In contrast, STSM uses stochasticity in a fundamentally different way. In STSM, randomness is often introduced to represent actual ecological uncertainty, such as wildfire ignition, insect outbreaks, disturbance spread, harvest sequencing, or future landscape processes. The stochastic component therefore reflects uncertainty in the behavioUr of the forest system itself. In Patchworks, the stochastic component primarily reflects uncertainty in the optimization search procedure rather than uncertainty in forest dynamics. Put simply, stochasticity in Patchworks helps the model find a solution, whereas stochasticity in STSM helps represent the uncertainty of the real world.

This distinction is important when interpreting results. A Patchworks solution is generally viewed as a near-optimal harvest schedule generated through a stochastic search process. An STSM result is generally viewed as one realization of a possible future landscape trajectory, often requiring multiple simulation replications to characterize the range of potential outcomes. Consequently, repeated Patchworks runs are often performed to ensure optimization stability, while repeated STSM runs are often performed to quantify ecological uncertainty and risk

12.1.4 Forest Dynamics

The treatment of forest dynamics further illustrates the distinction between the systems. Patchworks uses stand-level growth and yield relationships and allows users to model treatments, succession pathways, harvesting activities, and transportation networks. Forest development is represented through attribute curves and treatment schedules associated with blocks and tracks. This approach is efficient for exploring harvest scheduling alternatives and tactical planning questions.

STSM adopts a broader landscape ecology perspective. The model integrates stand aging, succession, natural disturbance agents, forest cover objectives, road dynamics, transfers between land-base categories, and management priorities. Because these processes are explicitly represented as interacting landscape events, STSM is especially suited to examining long-term ecosystem dynamics and cumulative effects. The framework was specifically developed to integrate timber supply analysis with broader environmental considerations such as biodiversity, habitat, climate change, and natural disturbance regimes.

A key implication of these differing approaches is the scale at which forest change is evaluated. In Patchworks, individual stands called blocks form the primary units of analysis.A block represents a relatively homogeneous area of forest that is treated as a single decision-making unit during the harvest scheduling process. Rather than scheduling individual inventory polygons independently, Patchworks groups one or more polygons together into blocks that can be harvested, managed, and analyzed as a cohesive unit. This approach better reflects how forestry operations are implemented on the ground, where harvesting and silvicultural treatments are typically applied to operational units rather than individual inventory polygons

The model places considerable emphasis on determining when and where harvesting should occur, how treatments influence future yields, and how tactical considerations such as access, transportation, and management constraints affect scheduling decisions. Forest dynamics are represented primarily to support harvest allocation and wood supply objectives, allowing modellers and planners to assess the consequences of alternative management strategies on future timber production. This makes Patchworks particularly effective for strategic and tactical planning exercises where the primary focus is balancing wood supply targets with economic and policy constraints.

In contrast, STSM places greater emphasis on understanding how multiple ecological and management processes interact over time to shape landscape conditions. Forest development is not viewed solely through the lens of timber production but as part of a larger socio-ecological system in which harvesting, wildfire, insect outbreaks, road development, succession, and policy objectives continuously influence one another. Because these processes operate within a shared spatial state space, STSM can capture feedbacks that emerge across large areas and long time horizons. For example, wildfire may alter age-class distributions, which in turn affect harvest opportunities, habitat availability, future disturbance susceptibility, and long-term growing stock. This ability to represent interacting landscape processes makes STSM particularly valuable for cumulative effects assessments, landscape resilience studies, climate adaptation analysis, and ecosystem-based management where understanding long-term change is often as important as estimating future timber supply.

12.1.5 Natural Disturbance

Another important difference lies in their treatment of natural disturbance. Patchworks was designed primarily around forest management optimization and operational scheduling. Although ecological objectives can be incorporated into planning targets, natural disturbances are not the central organizing principle of the model. STSM, on the other hand, places natural disturbance at the core of landscape projection. Disturbance agents such as wildfire and mountain pine beetle can be represented explicitly, including interactions with harvesting and future landscape conditions. Climate-change-related disturbances and complex disturbance regimes can also be incorporated through advanced model components. This capability makes STSM particularly valuable for modern forest management situations where disturbance dynamics are major drivers of future forest conditions.

A limitation of the Patchworks optimization framework arises directly from one of its greatest strengths: the ability to evaluate the entire planning horizon simultaneously. Because Patchworks seeks an optimal harvest allocation across all future periods at once, it effectively has complete knowledge of the future conditions that are represented within the modelling problem. The scheduler can evaluate harvest opportunities, fiber availability, transportation requirements, and management constraints over the entire planning horizon when determining the best solution. While this provides powerful optimization capabilities, it creates challenges when attempting to model natural disturbances as truly dynamic and spatially explicit future events.

In reality, natural disturbances such as wildfire, insect outbreaks, windthrow, or disease occur unpredictably in both time and space. Forest managers do not know which stand will burn fifty years from now, which watershed will experience a mountain pine beetle outbreak, or which portion of the landscape will remain unaffected. A truly spatially explicit disturbance model must therefore operate under uncertainty and allow disturbance patterns to emerge through time. Because Patchworks optimizes across the entire planning horizon, future disturbances cannot easily be treated as unknown events without fundamentally altering the optimization process. If future disturbances are explicitly specified within the modelling framework, the optimizer effectively “knows” where those disturbances will occur and can adjust harvest schedules accordingly. This creates a form of perfect foresight that does not exist in real-world forest management.

For example, suppose a wildfire is programmed to occur in a particular operating area during Year 80 of a simulation. Since the Patchworks scheduler is evaluating all periods simultaneously, it may identify stands that will eventually burn and schedule them for harvest beforehand because doing so improves the overall objective function. In effect, the optimization process is able to harvest timber before the disturbance occurs because it has foreknowledge of future events. While this behavior is rational from an optimization perspective, it can produce outcomes that are unrealistic from an ecological or tactical standpoint because real managers would never have certainty regarding the exact timing and location of future disturbances decades in advance. The result can be an underestimation of disturbance impacts and an overestimation of the ability of management actions to mitigate future losses.

This issue is one reason why natural disturbance in Patchworks has traditionally been represented using non-recoverable loss factors rather than through explicit landscape simulation. By treating disturbance as an expected reduction in timber supply rather than a specific future event, the model avoids the problem of allowing the optimizer to anticipate and respond to precise disturbance locations. The non-recoverable loss approach acknowledges that some portion of the forest will be lost to disturbance without requiring the model to specify exactly where or when those losses will occur. Although this sacrifices spatial realism, it preserves the integrity of the optimization framework and provides a practical means of accounting for disturbance risk in timber supply analyses.

STSM was specifically designed to overcome this challenge through its step-wise simulation approach. Because the model progresses sequentially from one time step to the next, future disturbances are not known in advance and instead emerge as the simulation unfolds. A wildfire occurring in Year 80 cannot influence harvest decisions made in Year 20 because the disturbance has not yet occurred within the simulation. Landscape conditions evolve through the interaction of succession, harvesting, road development, and disturbance processes over time, creating feedbacks that more closely resemble those observed in real forest ecosystems. As a result, STSM is generally better suited for examining disturbance dynamics, cumulative effects, and landscape resilience under uncertain future conditions, whereas Patchworks remains particularly effective for optimization-based harvest scheduling under assumed planning conditions.

In many ways, the distinction can be summarized as perfect foresight versus emergent futures. Patchworks optimizes with knowledge of all conditions represented within the planning horizon, allowing it to identify highly efficient harvest schedules but making truly stochastic, spatially explicit disturbance modelling difficult. STSM, by progressing sequentially through time, allows future disturbances to emerge naturally and influence the landscape only after they occur. Consequently, Patchworks excels at finding the best solution given known conditions, while STSM excels at exploring how uncertain ecological processes shape future forest conditions and timber supply opportunities over time.

12.1.6 Roads

The representation of transportation and access provides another contrast. Patchworks includes sophisticated transportation modelling capabilities, allowing simultaneous optimization of harvest allocations and haul networks. The model can evaluate multiple products, multiple destinations, road construction alternatives, transportation costs, and mill delivery strategies. This operational focus allows planners to estimate delivered wood costs and evaluate roadless-area options directly within the harvest scheduling framework.

STSM also incorporates roads, but with a different purpose. Roads are modelled as part of a dynamic landscape process rather than purely an economic transportation network. Road segments may be activated, built, deactivated, and reactivated in response to harvesting activity. Road access influences harvest availability, while road development creates feedbacks that affect future harvesting opportunities and landscape conditions. Thus, road networks in STSM are part of the broader ecological and cumulative-effects system rather than a primarily transportation-focused optimization problem.

The differing treatment of roads reflects the primary objectives of each modelling platform. In Patchworks, roads are fundamentally viewed as infrastructure that enables the movement of timber from harvest areas to processing facilities. Transportation efficiency therefore becomes a key component of the optimization process. The model can evaluate alternative road-building strategies, haul distances, product flows, and mill demands while simultaneously determining harvest schedules. This allows analysts to assess not only whether timber can be harvested sustainably, but also whether it can be delivered economically. As a result, Patchworks is particularly effective for examining operational questions such as minimizing delivered wood costs, evaluating alternative mill supply strategies, and determining the economic implications of road construction and access decisions.

In STSM, roads are viewed less as an economic network and more as a process that alters the landscape and influences future management opportunities. Road construction can expand access into previously unavailable areas, while road deactivation can reduce future accessibility and affect subsequent harvest patterns. Because road networks interact with forest cover, disturbance processes, priorities, and management objectives, they become an integral component of the model’s representation of landscape change. This allows STSM to evaluate questions that extend beyond transportation efficiency, such as how expanding access affects habitat conditions, how road density changes over time, how roads interact with disturbance and recovery processes, and how cumulative road development influences long-term landscape sustainability. Consequently, roads in STSM serve both as a management tool and as an ecological driver within the broader forest landscape system.

12.1.7 User Interaction

User interaction also differs significantly between the platforms. Patchworks provides a highly interactive graphical interface that combines GIS visualization with real-time scheduling. Users can view maps, edit treatments, adjust targets, inspect reports, and immediately observe the impacts of changes. STSM operates more like a modelling framework than an interactive planning tool. Users typically work through structured scenario scripts, parameter files, spatial inputs, tables, and SELES model components. Timber supply analysis involves configuring experiments, running simulations, reviewing outputs, and iteratively refining scenarios. Although STSM produces both spatial and tabular outputs, its workflow is generally more technical and script-driven than Patchworks.

These differences result in very different user experiences. Patchworks is designed to support modellers and planners who may wish to interact directly with the model while exploring alternatives. Through its graphical interface, users can adjust target weights, review objective performance, examine harvest blocks, test operational decisions, and visualize outcomes in near real time. This interactive environment can be particularly valuable during collaborative planning exercises, stakeholder consultations, or management workshops where participants want immediate feedback on how changes to objectives or constraints affect model outcomes. The ability to interactively explore trade-offs through the user interface makes Patchworks accessible to a broad range of forestry practitioners, including planners who may have limited programming or scripting experience.

STSM, in contrast, is generally better suited to analysts who are comfortable working with model configuration files, scenario scripts, and large collections of spatial and tabular inputs. Rather than adjusting objectives through graphical controls, users typically modify scenario parameters, input tables, harvest targets, disturbance assumptions, or management rules and then execute a series of simulations to evaluate the resulting outcomes. This workflow requires greater technical familiarity but provides substantially more flexibility for representing complex ecological processes and custom management scenarios. As a result, STSM is often used in research, timber supply reviews, cumulative effects assessments, and landscape-level planning exercises where analytical rigor, transparency, and model customization are prioritized over interactive real-time decision support.

12.1.8 Software Availability and Licensing

Another notable difference between the two platforms is their approach to software licensing and accessibility. Patchworks is a commercial software product developed and distributed by Spatial Planning Systems. Organizations wishing to use Patchworks must purchase software licenses and typically maintain an ongoing maintenance and support agreement to receive software updates, technical support, and access to new functionality. As a proprietary system, access to the software, source code, and certain technical components is controlled by the vendor. The commercial model provides users with dedicated vendor support, formal training opportunities, and a well-established software development and maintenance framework.

STSM, in contrast, is implemented within the SELES modelling framework and is distributed as an openly available modelling system. Users can download the software, documentation, and associated modelling tools without the need to purchase commercial licenses. This open-access approach lowers barriers to adoption for government agencies, researchers, academics, Indigenous organizations, consultants, and students who may have limited software budgets. The availability of the underlying modelling framework also encourages greater transparency, customization, peer review, and methodological development, allowing users to modify model structures, develop new applications, and adapt the platform to emerging research and management challenges.

The difference in licensing reflects broader differences in philosophy between the two systems. Patchworks evolved as a commercial decision-support product focused on providing an integrated, user-friendly optimization environment supported by a dedicated software company. STSM and SELES evolved primarily within a research, government, and cumulative-effects assessment environment, emphasizing flexibility, transparency, and extensibility. Consequently, organizations often choose between the platforms not only based on modelling capabilities but also on considerations such as licensing costs, technical support requirements, opportunities for customization, institutional expertise, and long-term software sustainability.

12.2 Conclusion

Despite these differences, the two systems share several important similarities. Both are spatially explicit, both support long-term planning horizons, both incorporate forest growth and succession processes, and both allow evaluation of alternative management scenarios. Both generate spatial and tabular outputs, support timber supply analysis, and help modellers and planners assess the consequences of management decisions over long time horizons. Most importantly, both seek to balance timber production objectives with broader ecological and social considerations.

In conclusion, Patchworks and STSM represent two complementary approaches to forest modelling. Patchworks is best characterized as an interactive optimization and harvest scheduling tool capable of balancing multiple objectives while producing operationally realistic harvest and transportation plans. STSM is best viewed as a simulation-based forest landscape and cumulative effects assessment framework that emphasizes dynamic interactions among ecological processes, natural disturbances, roads, and management actions. Patchworks excels in exploring trade-offs and identifying efficient harvest strategies, while STSM excels in understanding long-term landscape dynamics and sustainable timber supply under complex ecological conditions. Together, they illustrate the evolution of forest modelling from traditional wood-supply planning toward integrated ecosystem-based management and cumulative effects analysis.
